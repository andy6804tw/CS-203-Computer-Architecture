Division by Reciprocation On-line Help

This document  outlines the steps performed during the division of two fractions. It then shows what each of the entries in the simulator expects  giving a suitable example.
 
 

Steps involved in performing Division by Reciprocation:

The  reciprocal of the divisor, D is first calculated and then multiplied by the dividend, N to form the final quotient.

The reciprocal of D is calculated using the Newton-Raphson Iteration

                xi+1 = xi(2 - D*xi)            ----------------- (1)

and the initial guess x0 is obtained from a ROM table.

NOTE :  Let D = 0.1xxxxxxxabcd
The  'j' most significant bits of D are  the 'j' bits after 0.1. In the above example, the 7 most significant bits of D are   all the x.

1. Divide [0.5, 1) into 2j  intervals.

2. Using the 'j' most significant bits of D ('j' bits after 0.1) determine which interval(k) the 'j' significant bits of D fall  into.

3. The initial guess is generated by            x0(k) = 2j+1/(2j + k - 1/2)

4. Using eq (1), we  iterate until two successive values of xi are equal or to a maximum of log2n steps whichever is lesser, where n is the number of bits in the operands and the result. This value of xi  gives the desired 1/D.
 
 
 

 Details of entries

Entry 1:  Enter a fraction  in either decimal or binary notation.
                eg. N: 0.85

Entry 2: Enter a normalized fraction(i.e. choose  D from the range [0.5, 1.0 ) )
                eg. D: 0.6

Buttons:
                Click on bin/dec to indicate the representation of your entries.
                In our example: dec

Entry 3: Enter the # of bits you wish to use for the binary representaion of N and D.
                e.g.,  No. of bits: 12

                Now, our N has  the binary representation : 0.110110011001
                Our D has the binary representation           : 0.100110011001

Entry 4: No. of bits of D after .1 that you wish to use for ROM Table lookup
                e.g., 8
 
                D = 0.100110011 is considered for ROM Table lookup.
                Dividing  [0.5, 1) into 28   = 256 intervals, we find that D lies in the interval, k = 26.

                Initial guess, x0   =  1.111100
 
Entry 5: No. of bits after the decimal point in x0 .
                In the above example, 6.

                The size of the ROM in this example is 28x6.
